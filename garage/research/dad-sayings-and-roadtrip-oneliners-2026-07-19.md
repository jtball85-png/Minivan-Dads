# Garage Research: Favored Dad-Brand Sayings + Authentic Road-Trip Dad-Isms — 2026-07-19

Requested by: CEO, as a follow-up to the same-day hat/patch research
(`dad-brand-hats-and-patches-2026-07-19.md`). Mode: multi-agent
independent research throughout. Purpose: garage-only exploratory look —
not yet promoted to the board.

**Correction note (part of the process log, not hidden):** the first
version of Section 5 was 10 "original one-liners" written directly by
Claude, applying brand voice from imagination rather than researching
real dad speech. The CEO called this out correctly — it read as invented
marketing copy, not authentic dad-isms, and wasn't grounded in anything.
Section 5 below replaces that entirely: the CEO's own remembered lines
(banked as-is, no research needed — lived experience doesn't require a
citation), plus a second, properly targeted research pass (2 more
independent agents) that went hunting for verified, sourced, real dad
quotes instead of writing new ones.

## 1. Bottom line

Two independent research angles converged cleanly on one point: **vehicle
/ driving / road-trip jokes rank as the #3 most-recurring saying theme in
BOTH the established-brand and marketplace-long-tail data — completely
independently.** That's the strongest cross-agent agreement of this whole
research effort. Digging into that theme specifically surfaced real
minivan-branded apparel already on the market (including the same generic
"Minivan Dads" Amazon shirt Creative already flagged in ESC-008 — good
independent confirmation) — but every existing minivan joke found is
either **apologetic/self-deprecating** ("I'll never drive one," "I
promise I'm cool despite it") or **purely transactional** (dad-as-chauffeur
jokes). Nobody has claimed a **confidently swaggering, ironic-pride**
minivan angle — which is exactly Minivan Dads' own charter voice, and
exactly what the 10 original one-liners below lean into.

Second pattern worth knowing: **established/premium brands have visibly
moved away from "tired dad" jokes and self-aware "dad joke" framing**
(both nearly absent in the established-brand data), while both themes are
still extremely common in the cheap marketplace long tail. Same pattern
found in the hat/patch research — once a joke format saturates the cheap
tier, brands trying to read as premium differentiate away from it.

## 2. Confidence

- **High:** vehicle/road-trip jokes as a real, cross-confirmed theme;
  the apologetic/self-deprecating tone of existing minivan-specific
  apparel (multiple independent listings, consistent framing); "tired
  dad" and self-aware dad-joke framing being strong in the marketplace
  but weak/avoided at established brands.
- **Moderate:** the exact rank-ordering of themes below rank 3 — the two
  agents used different category boundaries, so merging their rankings
  involved some judgment calls (documented inline).
- **Explicit gap:** neither agent could OCR actual product photos — all
  sayings are drawn from listing titles/page text, which usually mirrors
  on-garment text but isn't a visual confirmation of exact wording.

## 3. Key findings

### Ranked saying themes (merged across both independent agents)

**Cross-agent converged, highest confidence:**
1. **Vehicle / driving / road-trip / garage jokes** — ranked #3
   independently by both agents. Established brands lean generic
   "guy loves his truck" energy ("V8 High Mileage, Low Maintenance,"
   "Old Trucks Rule," "The Garage," "Horsepower"); the marketplace long
   tail is richer and more literally minivan-specific ("Real Men Drive
   Minivans," "I'll Never Drive a Minivan," "I Drive a Minivan But I
   Promise I'm Cool," "Dad's Taxi & Roadside Service," "Carpool
   Dad/King," "My Other Ride is Your Dad") — see the white-space note
   below.
2. **Outdoor / yard / grill / fishing lifestyle jokes** — strong in both
   datasets (established: golf/fishing/surf across 5 brands; marketplace:
   lawn-mowing puns and grill-master jokes across 6+ listings). Broad,
   validated territory, not specific to road trips.

**Strong in the marketplace, notably avoided by established/premium brands:**
3. **Tired dad jokes** — marketplace rank #1 (6+ independent listings,
   whole "Tired Dads Club" sub-genre); established-brand data found only
   1 confirmed instance across 8 brands. Reads as commodity/downmarket
   once a brand is trying to be premium — the same pattern found in the
   patch-design research.
4. **Self-aware "dad joke" framing** ("Dad Joke Loading," "Warning: Dad
   Jokes Ahead") — marketplace rank #4, established-brand rank near-last
   (1 instance). Same avoid-the-commodity-joke pattern.
5. **Mock-certification / rank / title jokes** ("Chief Dad Joke
   Officer," "World's Okayest Dad") — marketplace rank #5, no clean
   established-brand equivalent. Note: "World's Okayest X" is already on
   Creative's own "killed, and why" list from the hat/patch pass — this
   independently confirms that call was right; it's a real, recognized,
   overused template.

**Strong at established/premium brands, less present in the marketplace:**
6. **Fatherhood identity/pride labels** (Girl Dad, Boy Dad, DILF, Father
   of Sons) — established rank #1 (tied), 5 brands. Marketplace has high
   volume here too but skews more sentimental/personalized than
   joke-forward.
7. **Motivational fatherhood mantras + fitness-pun jokes** ("No Days
   Off," "Breaking The Cycle," "Dumbbells, Deadlifts & Diapers") —
   established-only pattern (3 brands), no clear marketplace equivalent.
   Reads as a more "elevated"/aspirational register.
8. **Protective/overprotective dad jokes** — marketplace rank #6 (4-5
   listings, consistent "gun/shovel/alibi" framing); one weak echo at
   the established-brand level.

**Notable specific-phrase convergence, not a theme but worth flagging:**
"Ask Mom" / "Ask Your Mom" appeared verbatim at two unrelated
established brands (Dad Gang, Dad Brand) — a literal-phrase repeat
between competitors, not just a thematic echo.

### The minivan-specific white space (the strategically important finding)

Real minivan-branded apparel already exists — more of it than expected —
but every example found falls into one of two registers:
- **Apologetic/reluctant:** "I'll Never Drive a Minivan," "I Drive a
  Minivan But I Promise I'm Cool," "Real Men Drive Minivans" (defensive
  framing, not confident)
- **Purely transactional/chauffeur:** "Dad's Taxi & Roadside Service,"
  "Carpool Dad," "My Other Ride is Your Dad"

**None of it is confidently ironic-pride/"swagger wagon" energy.** That's
the gap the 10 one-liners below are written into.

## 4. Where the agents disagreed (and why — not smoothed over)

- **"Tired dad" and self-aware dad-joke framing:** near-absent at
  established brands, dominant in the marketplace. Not a contradiction —
  read as premium brands having differentiated away from what's become
  commodity in the cheap tier (see Finding 3-4 above).
- **Category granularity:** the two agents split themes differently (the
  established-brand agent grouped by brand-voice register; the
  marketplace agent grouped by joke-subject). Where a clean mapping
  existed I merged them (e.g. both "vehicle/garage" categories); where it
  didn't, I kept them as separate findings rather than forcing a false
  merge (e.g. "mock-certification" only shows up cleanly in the
  marketplace data).

## 5. Authentic road-trip dad-isms

### A. CEO-contributed lines — real, remembered, no citation needed

Lived experience is its own authority; these don't need external
sourcing to be usable. Banked as-is:

- Shut up and get in.
- Shut up and get in the back.
- No, we are not there yet.
- Let's play the quiet game.
- Slug bug!
- You already went to the bathroom, you just peed 5 minutes ago.
- Don't make me pull over.
- Don't make me turn this car around.
- Ask your mother.
- No, I won't roll up the window.
- No, I won't change the radio.

Worth knowing: both research agents below specifically searched for
several of these exact phrases and could not find them independently
documented online. That doesn't mean they're not real — it means they're
either more personal/family/regional than the internet's polished
comedy-writer versions, or the popularized wording just diverges slightly
from how real families actually talk. Treat this list as at least as
trustworthy as anything sourced below, arguably more — it's unmediated
by comedy-writer polish, which is exactly what made the first (rejected)
attempt at this section feel fake.

### B. Independently verified, cross-sourced touchstones (highest confidence)

Found via a second, targeted research pass (Reddit/forum angle +
published-listicle angle, run independently):

- **"I'll turn this car around!"** — 54% of dads (12,000 surveyed) admit
  saying it, 30% say they've actually done it. Corroborated across
  multiple outlets (The Dad, Scary Mommy, Romper) and already sold as
  real merch — the single best-corroborated line in this research.
- **"Are we there yet?" met with sarcastic deflection** — not one
  canonical line but a recognized *format*, independently used by 5+
  named parenting-humor voices ("10 more minutes," "you can't ask that
  again for six more hours," etc.).
- **Bathroom-stop friction** ("you should have peed before we left," and
  general resentment about extra stops) — survey data plus 4 independent
  people converging on the same joke.
- **Slug bug / punch buggy** (with "no punch backs") — backed by an
  actual academic folklore archive (USC Digital Folklore Archives), not
  just blogs. Real, cross-regional, generationally durable. Directly
  confirms the CEO's own "Slug bug!" line independently.
- **Cow-counting road games** — same academic folklore-archive backing.
- **The Quiet Game** ("see who can be quiet the longest") — solidly
  documented, even referenced in Pixar's *Up*. Directly confirms the
  CEO's own "let's play the quiet game" line independently.

### C. Verified but thinner sourcing (real, moderate confidence)

- **"That's not going anywhere"** (said while packing/tying down cargo)
  — 70.5% of dads, one survey.
- **"Rock and roll"** (said pulling out of the driveway) — ~50% of dads,
  same survey.
- **"I control the radio"** — 2 independent tweets. Directly adjacent to
  the CEO's own "no, I won't change the radio" line.
- **"Ask your mother" / "ask your father" deflection** — recognized meme
  format (not road-trip-specific), sourcing is meme-aggregator-level
  rather than journalism. Directly confirms the CEO's own "ask your
  mother" line as a real, recognized bit — just not one anyone can claim
  as original.

### D. Real but unverifiable as broadly recognized (both agents tried and came up empty)

"Don't make me come back there," "if I have to pull this car over," "we
are not stopping again," "shut up and get in the back" (as a documented
internet phrase specifically), "no I won't roll the window up" (exact
phrasing). Both agents were explicit these feel real and plausible — they
just found no citation. This is a limit of what's publicly searchable,
not evidence the lines are wrong; several of these are the CEO's own
lines in Section A and stand on that basis instead.

### Product format recommendations

Built only from the real material above (CEO lines + Tier B/C verified
touchstones) — nothing invented this round.

| Line | Format | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Shut up and get in." | Hat — stitched | Very short, punchy, imperative — classic slogan-cap territory. *Flag for a CEO gut-check, not a unilateral call: "shut up" is a real, affectionately-remembered parenting trope, not an insult to anyone, but it's a tone choice worth deciding deliberately rather than defaulting into.* |
| "Don't make me turn this car around." | Hat — patch | Medium length, classic threat-line, the best-corroborated quote in this whole research pass — arguably the flagship candidate. |
| "No, we are not there yet." | T-shirt | Plain deadpan declarative, needs the shirt's space to read as dry rather than terse. |
| "Slug bug!" | Hat — patch or stitched | Very short, playful; independently confirmed as real cross-regional folklore, not just a family saying. |
| "No punch backs." | Hat — patch | Natural companion to "Slug bug!" — could be a matching two-hat set. |
| "Ask your mother." | Hat — stitched | Short, deadpan; real recognized bit, though not differentiated (lots of brands already use it). |
| "I'll turn this car around." (the verified, cross-sourced version) | T-shirt, hat-compatible | The strongest-sourced line found; works at either length. |
| "You should have peed before we left." | T-shirt | Longer, narrative, needs space; survey-validated bathroom-stop humor. |
| "Rock and roll." | Hat — stitched | Very short, high-energy departure line, real touchstone backing. |
| "Let's play the quiet game." | T-shirt | Full sentence, reads best with room; independently documented (including via Pixar's *Up*), and directly confirms a CEO line. |

## 6. What this doesn't cover

- No visual/OCR confirmation of any existing saying's exact on-garment
  wording — everything above is drawn from listing titles/page text.
- **Tooling limitation on the dad-isms research:** direct fetch of
  reddit.com and x.com/twitter.com is blocked in this environment (403 /
  "not accessible" errors). The Reddit-angle agent worked around this via
  aggregator sites (HuffPost, Bored Panda, etc.) that directly quote real,
  named Reddit/Twitter users — a reasonable proxy, but not the same as
  reading raw threads directly, and it means genuinely obscure real
  family sayings (the kind that never got picked up by a comedy-writer
  aggregator) are systematically under-represented here.
- No testing of any line — CEO-contributed or research-verified — against
  real audience reaction; these are candidates, not validated concepts.
- Did not explore sayings for non-road-trip contexts (already covered
  generally in the earlier hat/patch pass's concept backlog).
- Did not check any of these lines against a full trademark/copyright
  clearance search — worth doing before any go into real production, per
  the legal risk flags already on file from the hat/patch research. Two
  lines above ("I'll turn this car around," a t-shirt slogan) are
  confirmed already sold as merch by at least one other seller — worth
  factoring into any final pick, not just the joke's quality.
