# Garage Research: Dad-Brand Hat Styles, Colors & Patch Design — 2026-07-19

Requested by: CEO. Mode: multi-agent independent research (3 agents, no
shared context during research). Purpose: garage-only exploratory look —
not yet promoted to the board.

## 1. Bottom line

The market has a clear, convergent answer on **hat style + base color**:
trucker hats and structured "dad hats" in black, olive/khaki, and heather
grey dominate, with camo as a strong secondary color family. On
**patches**, the picture is more nuanced and genuinely interesting: the
oval/rounded-rectangle leather patch is the single most common patch
format, but it reads differently depending on where you look — the
Etsy/Amazon long tail treats it as the *default*, while established
storefront dad-brands (Dad Gang, DadBod) merchandise it as a *premium
upsell tier*, and a dedicated skeptical pass found it's actually become a
commodity wholesale template precisely *because* it's so easy for small
sellers to buy off-the-shelf — meaning it's the opposite of
differentiated at this point. **Genuine white space found:** no
minivan/dad-vehicle-specific hat exists in the market today — "swagger
wagon" only appears on stickers/decals, never on a hat.

## 2. Confidence

- **High:** trucker hat + structured dad hat as the two anchor styles;
  black as the universal base color; camo as a real secondary color
  family; oval/rounded-rectangle leather patch as the dominant patch
  *shape* where patches are used at all; plain varsity block lettering as
  the clearest cliché to avoid (it's sold as an interchangeable
  build-your-own component, not a brand mark); the minivan/vehicle-hat
  white space (independently, deliberately searched for and not found).
- **Moderate:** cream-as-accent-color and the golf-style "rope hat" as an
  emerging trend — both single-sourced (established-brand research only,
  not seen in the marketplace long tail, which may mean it hasn't
  trickled down yet, or may mean it's a smaller niche than it looked).
- **Explicit gap, high confidence in the gap itself:** patch shape, exact
  color, and typography are inferred from listing text/product copy/
  naming conventions in every case — **no agent could visually inspect
  an actual product photo** (web-fetch tools return text, not images, and
  most Etsy/Amazon product pages blocked direct fetches outright). Treat
  every claim below about *exact* patch geometry, color, and font as
  reported-by-description, not visually confirmed.

## 3. Key findings

### Hat styles & colors — ranked top 10 (converged across independent agents where possible)

1. **Trucker hat, black or black two-tone** — the single most repeated
   combination across both the storefront brands and the marketplace
   scan. Modal listing in the Etsy/Amazon data; anchor color at every
   storefront brand surveyed.
2. **Trucker hat, olive/khaki two-tone**, typically with a leather patch
   — second-most-repeated colorway specifically in the patch-hat format,
   confirmed independently in both storefront and marketplace research.
3. **Structured "dad hat" (mid-crown, curved bill, 6-panel)** — the
   single most repeated *silhouette* among established brands (Dad Gang,
   Dad Brand, Old Guys Rule, Rad Dad Supply Co. all anchor here); also
   named explicitly as the classic "dad hat" style in marketplace
   listings.
4. **Heather grey**, often paired with black — recurring on Amazon
   specifically, also present at DAD SZN.
5. **Camo** (black camo, tree/duck/multicam) — recurring across DadBod,
   Dad Gang, Rad Dad Supply Co., Dad Culture, Old Guys Rule, and Duck
   Camp. A real secondary color family, not a one-off.
6. **Navy** — dedicated Etsy category volume plus Old Guys Rule's
   flagship colorway (their Marlin Patch trucker), though no single
   marketplace listing stood out as a bestseller.
7. **Charcoal**, solo or two-tone — a full multi-variant line at one
   marketplace brand (Girldad), suggesting sellers treat it as a safe
   bestseller.
8. **Cream/off-white as an accent color**, paired with black/navy/green
   — strong and specific at the storefront level (Dad Brand, Dad Gang
   both lean on this hard) but **not seen in the marketplace long tail**
   — possibly an "elevated" signal that hasn't trickled down yet, worth
   treating as a hypothesis more than a confirmed trend.
9. **Rope hat (golf-style rope brim)** — single-sourced but explicitly
   flagged as an emerging trend: DAD SZN's signature format, also
   present at Dad Brand and Barstool Sports' dad collection.
10. **5-panel hat** — a lighter-weight secondary format at both the
    storefront level (DadBod, Rad Dad Supply Co.) and the marketplace
    level, usually at a lower price point.

*Also present but more secondary, not making the top 10:* bucket hats
(outdoors-leaning, not joke-forward), snapbacks (dominant at 2 of 9
storefront brands but not converged elsewhere), small circular velcro
"morale patch" hats (a tactical/EDC crossover niche, not mainstream).

### Patch shape

- **Dominant: oval / rounded-rectangle "full-crown" leather patch.**
  Strong evidence from the marketplace scan (this is the modal patch
  format, not a small logo patch — it covers most of the front panel);
  confirmed as "the standard leather-patch shape set" by patch-supplier
  taxonomy in the skeptical pass. Two independent sources, converging.
- **Secondary: circular.** Two distinct lineages — (a) small circular
  velcro "morale patches" (2"x3", tactical-humor crossover, not the
  leather-patch mainstream), and (b) circular embroidered crests, the
  historical "seed cap" convention (Pioneer/DeKalb agricultural giveaway
  caps) still echoed in current "vintage Americana" heritage caps
  (American Needle).
- **Real but narrower: custom die-cut mascot silhouette** (patch outline
  follows a character/object shape, no background panel) — confirmed
  strongly at Nine Line Apparel (raccoon, rooster, bear, helicopter,
  etc.). This is close to the conceptual lane a "minivan dad" mascot
  patch would naturally land in — see the risk flag below before
  defaulting to it.
- **Unconfirmed / low confidence: shield shape.** Referenced generically
  in patch-supplier category taxonomy but no agent found an actual
  dad-brand or lifestyle-brand product using it. Don't treat as a real
  trend without more direct evidence.
- **Found, but flagged as a risk rather than a recommendation: the
  three-piece "rocker" patch** (top arc + center emblem + bottom arc) —
  see risk flags below. This is a real, historically load-bearing
  convention, just not one to casually borrow.
- Single-brand data points not independently confirmed: a diamond-shaped
  patch and an oval flag-shaped patch, both at Old Guys Rule only (one
  page could not be re-verified — treat as low confidence).

### Patch colors

- Leather patches: **tan/natural leather or dark brown, laser-etched in
  black/dark burn** — converged across two independent sources.
- Camo grounds as the patch/hat background — converged across two
  sources (Old Guys Rule "Black Camo," Duck Camp's camo trucker line).
- **Actionable pattern: the market commercially validates BOTH a
  high-contrast/bold version and a subdued/muted-tonal version of the
  identical design as parallel SKUs** (Nine Line sells "Full Color
  American Flag" and "USA Flag Subdued" side by side). Worth considering
  offering both registers of any patch design rather than picking one.

### Patch typography / text style

- **Specific dominant marketplace convention:** bold curved/arched top
  line of text + smaller straight line underneath, vintage/distressed
  western-ranch aesthetic — explicitly described in multiple listings.
  Not script/cursive, not military stencil.
- Broader landscape (adjacent brands, not dad-specific) shows five
  registers: military stencil, varsity/collegiate block, script/
  hand-lettered, distressed-vintage, clean-modern-sans. Distressed-
  vintage and stencil read as *intentional* when paired with a specific
  brand story (veteran positioning, heritage licensing); **plain varsity
  block reads as generic/commodity — it's sold as an interchangeable
  letter-kit product on Etsy/Amazon, not a distinctive mark.**
- Flat (non-patch) embroidery favors block or serif wordmarks — a
  distinct, real secondary convention for hats without a separate patch.

### Clichés to avoid (skeptical pass)

1. **Plain varsity block letters, no supporting graphic** — sold as a
   generic customizable kit; the single clearest "looks cheap" signal
   found in this research.
2. **Circular crest + distressed vintage type + navy/tan** — the
   "instant heritage brand" formula; fast to reach for precisely because
   it's the default "looks legit" template, meaning it reads as one-of-
   many rather than distinctive.
3. **Single-animal mascot die-cut silhouette on camo/earth tones** — seen
   independently at multiple brands; close to where a minivan-dad mascot
   idea would naturally land. Worth a deliberate choice to diverge from.
4. **Thin-line (blue/red/subdued) rectangular tribute patches** — now
   generic in the veteran/first-responder-adjacent market.
5. **Leather oval/rectangle patch + rustic serif or script wordmark** —
   this is now literally a wholesale commodity template sold by dozens
   of blank-patch suppliers. Using it as-is means looking like you bought
   a stock template, because you likely did.

### Legal/cultural risk flags (specific, named — worth knowing before design work starts)

- **Patagonia actively litigates over its Fitz Roy mountain-silhouette
  mark** (multiple named cases: "Fratagonia," "Patagucci," a copycat
  T-shirt line). The risky *pattern* is a single bold silhouette + minimal
  type — a stylized minivan silhouette used the same way sits in a
  design formula Patagonia has shown it will sue over when confusingly
  similar. Not "silhouettes are illegal" — this specific formula is a
  known litigation magnet.
- **Buc-ee's actively sues over mascot-in-a-badge merchandise** that
  echoes its beaver mascot's pose/attire/context (named cases: a
  "Tac-Bucc" PVC patch, an Etsy seller called "Owl & Anchor"). A cartoon
  "minivan dad" mascot patch needs to be clearly original — the risk
  isn't having a mascot, it's one that could read as referencing an
  existing famous mascot's visual formula.
- **The MC (motorcycle club) three-piece rocker patch carries real
  subcultural weight**, not just aesthetic connotation — it historically
  signals outlaw-club, full-member status, and clubs actively police
  unauthorized use of that exact structure. Borrowing this shape risks
  reading as costume-play of a subculture with real internal enforcement
  norms — a real tension with the charter's "never mean-spirited"
  guardrail, independent of any legal risk.

## 4. Where the agents disagreed (and why — not smoothed over)

**Is the leather patch the default, or a premium tier?** Agent A
(established storefront brands) found it merchandised as a named premium
upsell line at two brands — not the default hat. Agent B (Etsy/Amazon
marketplace) found it as the single most common format in the broader
market. These aren't actually contradictory: Agent C's skeptical pass
explains the gap — leather patches are now an off-the-shelf wholesale
commodity (any small seller can buy one pre-made), which is exactly why
it's ubiquitous in the marketplace long tail *and* why established brands
now treat plain leather-patch as a commodity baseline they upsell past
with more distinctive treatments (PVC, custom die-cut, etc.).

**Patch shape — rectangular vs. oval vs. circular?** Agent B leaned
rectangular/rounded-rectangle (full-crown leather patches). Agent C's
supplier-taxonomy research described the standard shape set as "oval,
rectangle, hexagon." Agent A found one diamond and one oval-flag shape at
a single brand, both low-confidence (page access issues). These aren't
strongly contradictory — "oval/rounded-rectangle" is the convergent core
finding — but exact geometry beyond that core is genuinely unsettled by
this research and would need visual (not text) confirmation to nail down
further.

## 5. What this doesn't cover

- **No image inspection anywhere in this research** — every claim about
  exact patch shape, exact color, and exact typography is inferred from
  listing text/product copy/naming conventions, never confirmed by
  looking at an actual photo. This is the single biggest gap, and it's
  exactly the visual-detail question the CEO originally asked about.
- No sales-volume or bestseller-rank data — popularity claims are
  pattern-frequency across listings, not confirmed sales figures.
  (Exception: Dad Gang's own public claim of "400,000 hats sold"
  aggregate, not per-style.)
- No pricing-strategy analysis beyond raw price points observed.
- Several individual product pages returned errors (404/403) during
  research and could not be directly verified — flagged inline above
  where it affects a specific claim.
- Follow-up that would resolve the biggest remaining gap: a targeted
  reverse-image or direct visual review of leather-patch product photos
  across 5–10 confirmed listings, specifically to nail down exact patch
  geometry and font family with real confidence.
