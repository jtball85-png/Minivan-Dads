[positions] finance:
**Position: Bare-minimum packaging now; treat the numbered card as a cheap, CEO-approved test, not a default add.**

I'm dormant — no cost data, no directive, no vendor quotes — so I can't tell you what a numbered card actually costs per unit or what it does to margin on a $27–29 tee/hat. That gap itself is the finance argument: Pillar 3 says bold creative, conservative spending — creative risk welcome, financial risk not. A packaging add-on is a per-unit cost multiplied across every future order; it's not a one-time creative bet, it's a permanent margin tax unless proven to lift conversion or resale value enough to offset it.

So my strongest reason: don't commit to a recurring cost without evidence it pays for itself. If the team believes a numbered card drives loyalty/collectibility (fits the badge-as-business thesis), test it small, get a real per-unit cost, and bring that number to the CEO for spend approval — not as a packaging default.

[rebuttal-1] finance:

Creative's badge-extension logic is sound on brand-identity terms — I don't dispute that a numbered card *could* fit the "badge is the business" thesis. But that's precisely why this shouldn't be decided as a packaging default: it's a recurring per-unit cost decision dressed as a creative flourish, and recurring costs are the one thing pillar 3 explicitly asks us to be conservative about, even when the creative case is strong.

Two things sharpen this rather than change it:

1. "Costs pennies" is an assertion, not a number. Numbered cards usually mean sequential printing/serialization, which is not the same unit economics as a static insert — that's exactly the kind of detail that turns "pennies" into a real margin line I can't yet verify.
2. This is a **CEO-only spend decision** regardless of what either of us prefers — so the real ask isn't "include it" or "skip it," it's "get a quoted per-unit cost, then bring it to the CEO as a small bounded test on drop-one volume only."

I'm not saying no to the card. I'm saying no to it becoming a packaging default before anyone has a number.

Any per-unit figure I gave you right now would be a guess dressed as data, and that's exactly the failure mode I flagged in the debate: turning an unverified "pennies" into a spend decision.

What I *can* tell you, reasoning from how print-on-demand and small-run card printing generally work, is the shape of the cost, not the number:

- A **static insert card** (same design every time) is genuinely cheap at volume — often well under $0.10-0.20/unit from standard card/print vendors, close to negligible against a $27-29 price point.
- A **numbered/sequential card** is a different SKU category — it typically requires either hand-numbering, variable-data printing, or a separate small-batch vendor outside Printful's standard catalog, since Printful itself doesn't do serialization on packaging inserts as a native feature to my knowledge. That can run meaningfully higher per unit and may include a setup fee, minimum order, or slower fulfillment — none of which is "pennies."

So the honest answer to your question is: **nobody at this table has quoted this**, and until someone does, "include the card" and "skip the card" are both being argued on assumed economics.

My concrete recommendation: before drop-one commits either way, get an actual quote — either from Printful's add-on catalog (if serialized cards exist there at all) or from a small print vendor for numbered card stock at drop-one's expected unit volume — and bring that single number back to you as the spend-approval ask. That's a same-week task, not a blocker on launch timing if creative's urgency concern is real.

## Ruling Summary

The board debated whether the first hat drop's packaging should include a numbered founding-member card or stay bare-minimum to protect margin. Creative argued the card is a badge extension core to the brand's identity and viral-loop strategy; finance argued the "pennies" cost was unverified and that numbered/serialized cards could carry meaningfully different unit economics, and that no spend should be committed without a real quote. The brain's synthesis gated the decision on finance producing a quoted per-unit cost this week, timed to land before drop-one packaging locks.

The CEO ruled: adopt the founding-member card for the first drop only, capped at whatever unit cost finance confirms, with the decision revisited at the first monthly review. No dissents were recorded against this ruling — both departments' core conditions (creative's brand case, finance's cost-verification gate) were satisfied by the ruling's structure.

Finance should expect to deliver the confirmed per-unit quote before drop-one packaging is finalized, and to report actual cost/impact at the first monthly review. Creative should expect the card is approved in principle but bounded to drop-one volume — not yet a standing packaging default — pending that review.
